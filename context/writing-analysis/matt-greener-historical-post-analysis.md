## 2. OPENING HOOK PATTERNS

### Pattern 1: The Provocative Question
Matt opens with a direct, challenging question that confronts industry assumptions.

**Verbatim Examples:**
- "Is the obsession with "more" killing marketing impact?"
- "Is Marketing dying at the hands of builders?"

**Structure:** `Is [industry assumption/trend] + [negative consequence framed as question]?`

---

### Pattern 2: The Industry Critique Statement
Matt calls out a celebrated practice and labels it problematic.

**Verbatim Examples:**
- "Our industry loves to celebrate a good "win-back" campaign. We treat it like a heroic last-minute victory."
- "Marketers are obsessed with "hyper-personalization." But beware!"
- "We have a new problem in marketing: Speed without soul."

**Structure:** `[Industry/Marketers] [loves/is obsessed with] + [practice]. + [But/contrarian pivot]`

---

### Pattern 3: The Dialogue Scene
Matt creates a mini-screenplay showing a dysfunctional conversation.

**Verbatim Example:**
```
CEO: "We need an AI strategy."

Marketer: "Right. Absolutely. I just saw a demo for..."

CEO: "What problem are we solving?"

Marketer: Silence. "...Efficiency?"

Both: "Let's try that new <insert hyped up on LinkedIn AI tool> that everyone's talking about."

This is the AI panic-buy.
```

**Structure:** Character dialogue → Awkward moment → Label the behavior

---

### Pattern 4: The Metaphor Reframe
Matt takes a familiar concept and redefines it through vivid metaphor.

**Verbatim Examples:**
- "Annual marketing plan? A masterpiece of spreadsheets and slide decks, presented with confidence in November... and utterly irrelevant by February."
- "GTM isn't a relay race. It's a three-legged race through a mud pit."

**Structure:** `[Common practice]? + [Sarcastic description] + [Metaphor pivot]`

---

### Pattern 5: The Personal Scene
Matt opens with a vivid, relatable personal observation.

**Verbatim Example:**
"The barista at my local coffee shop knows my order, asks about my weekend (if I'm lucky), and gives me a free coffee and samples from time to time (if I'm really lucky) not prompted by any specific milestone or anniversary."

**Structure:** Detailed sensory scene → Build emotional connection → Pivot to business insight

---

### Pattern 6: The Milestone Announcement
For company news, Matt uses emotional framing.

**Verbatim Example:**
"It's been a long time coming, years in the making actually. We've grown TINT and Zinrelo with a mission to return trust and authenticity to marketing."

**Structure:** `[Time statement] + [Mission framing] + [Vision statement]`

---

## 3. CLOSING PATTERNS

### Pattern 1: The Either/Or Question
Matt ends with a question that invites debate by presenting two viewpoints.

**Verbatim Examples:**
- "Do you agree, or are massive win-back efforts a necessary pillar of modern retention strategy?"
- "How do you balance productivity and function with brand and feeling?"

**Structure:** `Do you agree, or [alternative view]?` OR `How do you [balance/reconcile] X with Y?`

---

### Pattern 2: The Specific Problem Question
Matt asks readers to share their specific experience or challenge.

**Verbatim Examples:**
- "What's the one problem you're trying to solve first?"
- "What's on your 'stop-doing' list?"
- "What's the single biggest point of friction between your sales and marketing teams right now?"
- "What's the most off the mark "personalized" marketing you've seen? Extra points awarded for funny."

**Structure:** `What's [the one/your/the single biggest] + [specific challenge]?`

---

### Pattern 3: The Example Request
Matt invites readers to share brands or examples.

**Verbatim Examples:**
- "Is there a brand that is doing this well? That makes you feel like a "regular" What do they do to earn it?"
- "Have you seen any current B2B AI-generated content that's truly 'tasteful,' or is it all just the most-validated average?"

**Structure:** `Is there a brand/Have you seen + [specific criteria]?`

---

### Pattern 4: The Approach Question
Matt asks readers to share their methodology.

**Verbatim Example:**
- "What's your approach?"

## 5. SENTENCE STRUCTURE PATTERNS

### The Contrarian Pivot
**Pattern:** Statement of common belief → "But" → Challenge/reframe

**Verbatim Examples:**
- "We treat it like a heroic last-minute victory. Maybe it has to do with the cultural obsession with sports. But what if we're celebrating the wrong thing?"
- "Don't get me wrong—there is massive power in the build. Marketers who build move faster. [...] But are we over-indexing on prototyping and forgetting how to persuade?"

---

### The Triple Negative List
**Pattern:** Three "Stop [doing X]" commands with explanations

**Verbatim Example:**
```
Here are 3 things we must stop doing:

- Stop optimizing the garbage. The best segmentation in the world won't save a lukewarm campaign. AI doesn't fix a bad idea, it just distributes it faster.

- Stop chasing more at the expense of meaning. One genuinely surprising, well-crafted piece of content is worth 100 auto-generated blog posts. Commitment to the craft is what vaults you ahead, not scale.

- Stop replacing judgment with metrics. AI can tell you what happened, but only a human leader with instinct and market understanding can explain why it matters and what to do next.
```

---

### The Reframe Contrast
**Pattern:** "[Common view] isn't [what you think]. It's [real truth]."

**Verbatim Examples:**
- "A win-back isn't a success, it's proof that the brand<>customer relationship was already broken."
- "This isn't personalization. It's surveillance, sometimes with a coupon attached."
- "A product is a solution. A brand is a reason to care."
- "One is about being known, the other is about being tracked."

---

### The Question Cascade
**Pattern:** Rapid-fire rhetorical questions

**Verbatim Examples:**
- "If every marketer is a builder, who is left to be the storyteller?"
- "But are we over-indexing on prototyping and forgetting how to persuade? Are we obsessing over the function and ignoring the feeling?"
- "Instead of asking "Why did they leave?" we should be asking "Do they feel like leaving?" "Are they engaged enough to stay?""

---

### The Trifecta Framework
**Pattern:** Three-part conceptual framework with labels

**Verbatim Example:**
```
The new CMO mandate is now the Marketing Trifecta:

Strategy: The human-led North Star. Going a million kilometers an hour down the wrong road isn't a competitive advantage.

Velocity: Strategy without velocity is quickly overtaken, consumed, and lost in the noise.

Taste: The irreplaceable human filter for resonance. Velocity and strategy without taste fail to resonate with the target audience.
```

---

### The Brand Example Triple
**Pattern:** Three brand examples with emotional benefit, not feature

**Verbatim Example:**
```
➡️ Canva (Creative Confidence. Empowerment. Not just a design tool.)
➡️ Liquid Death (Defiance and Authenticity. Not just canned water.)
➡️ BMW (Freude am Fahren: "Joy in Driving". Not just an automobile.)
```

## 7. POST TEMPLATES WITH VERBATIM EXAMPLES

### Template 1: The Industry Myth Buster
**Structure:**
1. Industry loves [celebrated practice]
2. But what if we're celebrating the wrong thing?
3. [Practice] isn't [what they think] - it's [real truth]
4. The real work is [contrarian alternative]
5. Closing question inviting debate

**Full Verbatim Example - Win-Back Post:**
```
Our industry loves to celebrate a good "win-back" campaign. We treat it like a heroic last-minute victory. Maybe it has to do with the cultural obsession with sports. But what if we're celebrating the wrong thing? 🤔  A win-back isn't a success, it's proof that the brand<>customer relationship was already broken.

A win-back campaign is a frantic apology after the damage is done. The real work is in the "stay" campaign—the one that keeps your customer engaged continuously. It's about recognizing and acting on the digital equivalent of a sigh or an eye-roll weeks before your customer starts looking elsewhere. It's the shift from guesswork to foresight. Instead of asking "Why did they leave?" we should be asking "Do they feel like leaving?""Are they engaged enough to stay?"

The smartest brands are building relationships with their customers, continually giving them reasons to stay loyal. They're investing in predicting dissatisfaction, not just reacting to it.

Let's stop investing all our energy in resurrection and start focusing on preventative care.

Do you agree, or are massive win-back efforts a necessary pillar of modern retention strategy?
```

---

### Template 2: The AI Skeptic
**Structure:**
1. Name the new AI-era problem
2. Describe the seductive moment (velocity, efficiency)
3. But here's what's missing (human element)
4. Introduce a framework (usually 3 parts)
5. Land the insight about human value
6. Ask for examples/experience

**Full Verbatim Example - Speed Without Soul:**
```
We have a new problem in marketing: Speed without soul.

You know the moment. That giddy rush when you see your AI engine pump out 500 ad variations in a second. Velocity achieved. But now you're swimming in content that's perfectly optimized, perfectly targeted, and perfectly bland.

AI has solved the velocity problem. Great. The new CMO mandate is now the Marketing Trifecta:

Strategy: The human-led North Star. Going a million kilometers an hour down the wrong road isn't a competitive advantage.

Velocity: Strategy without velocity is quickly overtaken, consumed, and lost in the noise.

Taste: The irreplaceable human filter for resonance. Velocity and strategy without taste fail to resonate with the target audience.

AI is an exponential multiplier for average. You still need the finely tuned human intuition, to spot the gem that actually aligns with your brand's psychology.

Marketing today isn't about being the chief prompter. It's about being the chief curator of taste. Knowing when the data is lying and how to make the narrative feel human, not a robotic press release. That's the sustainable moat.

Have you seen any current B2B AI-generated content that's truly 'tasteful,' or is it all just the most-validated average?
```

---

### Template 3: The Personal Anecdote → Business Insight
**Structure:**
1. Paint a vivid personal scene (specific details)
2. Extract the universal truth from it
3. Contrast with how brands fail to replicate
4. Prescriptive advice ("Stop trying to X, instead Y")
5. Ask for brand examples

**Full Verbatim Example - Coffee Shop Community:**
```
The barista at my local coffee shop knows my order, asks about my weekend (if I'm lucky), and gives me a free coffee and samples from time to time (if I'm really lucky) not prompted by any specific milestone or anniversary. I know other regulars by sight, although not by name. We share a space, a ritual. That is a "community". Go to any local shop anywhere and you'll witness the same. No one forced it, no one planned it, it grew out of consistent personal recognition. This is the way that natural communities form, around shared interests and rituals.

Brands are desperate to replicate this feeling digitally, and most fail. They launch a site or gathering place, see a flurry of activity, and then watch it become a ghost town of disinterested, unengaged members. They forget that belonging isn't a feature you can ship. It's a feeling you have to earn, one transaction, one interaction, one personalized gesture at a time. The community isn't the starting point, it's the prize you win for exceptional loyalty.

Stop trying to "build a community" to force a community from scratch. Instead, create a loyalty program so good, so personalized and rewarding, that a community becomes the inevitable outcome. The feeling of being a "regular", of being an insider in a group can be the most potent force in marketing.

Is there a brand that is doing this well? That makes you feel like a "regular" What do they do to earn it?
```

### Template 4: The Metaphor Reframe
**Structure:**
1. Take a common practice and describe it sarcastically
2. Introduce unexpected metaphor
3. Expand the metaphor with specific details
4. Land the insight about agility/adaptation
5. Ask about reader's approach

**Full Verbatim Example - Sailing/Marketing Plan:**
```
Annual marketing plan? A masterpiece of spreadsheets and slide decks, presented with confidence in November... and utterly irrelevant by February.

Marketers spend weeks/months crafting this perfect, intricate roadmap for the next 12 months. But the market doesn't care about our Gantt charts. A competitor makes a move, 400 new competitors are spun up in 2 days via vibe coding, ChatGPT announces another breakthrough, and suddenly the big plan is a fossil—a relic of a bygone era (last quarter).

Don't treat planning like fixed architecture. Instead, treat it like sailing.

You don't map every wave for a year-long voyage. You set a destination (your big strategic goal), and then you navigate quarter by quarter, month by month, week by week, adjusting your sails to the actual wind and weather you encounter. The plan prepares you to navigate the future that you cannot predict.

What if we ditch the 12-month tactical blueprint and instead commit to strategic "missions" for the year, planned in agile 90-day sprints? What's your approach?

#MarketingPlan
```

### Template 5: The Dialogue + Diagnosis
**Structure:**
1. Create a mini-dialogue showing dysfunction
2. Label the behavior ("This is the X")
3. Diagnose the underlying fear/problem
4. Offer specific alternative approach
5. Ask the one-thing question

**Full Verbatim Example - AI Panic-Buy:**
```
CEO: "We need an AI strategy."

Marketer: "Right. Absolutely. I just saw a demo for..."

CEO: "What problem are we solving?"

Marketer: Silence. "...Efficiency?"

Both: "Let's try that new <insert hyped up on LinkedIn AI tool> that everyone's talking about."

This is the AI panic-buy.

We're so terrified of being left behind that we're buying shiny objects before we've even defined the problem. It's like buying a blender when all you need is a spoon (ironic much?).

One antidote to AI overload is starting with a smaller problem.

Before you try to boil the ocean, and before buying one more tool, find one, specific, annoying thing that's broken.

"Why do our 'loyal' customers only buy on deep discount?"

"What's the real reason people abandon our cart?"

Start there. Then (and only then) ask, "Can AI help us find the answer?"

What's the one problem you're trying to solve first?
```

1. Call out industry obsession directly
2. Warning/caution
3. Describe what it looks like when it fails
4. Reframe with contrast (being known vs being tracked)
5. Prescriptive advice
6. Ask for funny/specific examples

**Full Verbatim Example - Hyper-Personalization:**
```
Marketers are obsessed with "hyper-personalization." But beware! You might want to ensure that your customers aren't getting hyper-creeped out.

Real personalization is the difference between a server who remembers you don't like cilantro and an algorithm that knows you bought cilantro last Tuesday.

One is about being known, the other is about being tracked. The future doesn't belong to brands that track us best. It belongs to the ones that know us best. Stop chasing transactional data points and start building relationships that create contextual understanding.

The feeling of being a "regular", of being an insider in a group can be the most potent force in marketing.
