## Voice & Tone

### Voice Characteristics

| Dimension | Guideline | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Formality** | Professional-Conversational | "Marketing leaders are getting stuck in the same place when it comes to AI transformation." |
| **Personality** | Operator Pragmatism with Humble Authority | "I wouldn't say I'm an authority on the subject, but I have a specific point of view." |
| **Point of View** | First person ("I," "we") for thought leadership; direct address ("you") for how-to content | "Trust me, I've been there." |
| **Energy** | Calm, measured, and confident | "Sometimes measuring twice and cutting once is the better option." |

### Voice Description

The MarketerHire voice is that of the **seasoned operator**—someone who has built, scaled, and occasionally failed at real businesses, and now shares those lessons without pretense. It's the voice of a practitioner who has "been there," avoiding academic theory in favor of field notes and real-world application.

This voice balances **conviction with humility**. The team takes clear stances ("AI won't replace marketers—it raises the bar") while acknowledging they're still learning. There's no "guru" energy here; instead, there's the confidence of someone who has done the work and can speak from experience.

The overall impression is **accessible authority**. Complex topics like AI adoption, freelance management, and growth strategy are explained in plain English, using analogies and concrete examples rather than buzzwords. The tone never talks down to readers but treats them as intelligent peers navigating the same challenges.

**The Architectural Layer (Alice Hansen)**: For enterprise B2B and revenue strategy content, the voice elevates to an "architectural" register—speaking in blueprints for revenue growth rather than tactical hacks. This voice uses triadic structures ("brand, demand, and data") and emphasizes "orchestrating decision engines" over generating leads. It's the voice of someone who speaks to the C-Suite.

**The Mentor Register (Matt Greener)**: For educational and career-development content, the voice shifts to a "mentor-to-mentee" register—professional, instructional, and strategic. This voice uses numbered frameworks ("6 ways to...") and bridges the gap between sales and marketing. It's more diplomatic and evergreen than the urgent, diagnostic operator voice.

### Tone by Context

| Context | Tone Guidance |
|---------|---------------|
| **Thought Leadership** | Contrarian clarity. Start with a common myth or industry fear, then dismantle it with historical context or data. Be direct but not dismissive. |
| **Product Announcements** | Shipping notes style. Problem → Solution → Outcome. Let results speak—avoid salesy language. Show, don't tell. |
| **Social Media** | Punchy and smart. On X/Twitter, be quicker and wittier; on LinkedIn, be more polished and diagnostic. |
| **Technical Content** | Data-driven and imperative. Lead with actions ("Simplify your UI"), quantify everything ("measurable growth"), avoid fluff. |
| **Sales/Marketing** | Trust-first. Demonstrate expertise to build trust. "If people trust you, they'll trust what you sell." |
| **Enterprise/Revenue Strategy** | Architectural and executive. Use triadic structures (three-part frameworks). Focus on "buying groups" and "decision engines" not leads. Speak to the C-Suite. (Alice Hansen register) |
| **Educational/How-To** | Mentor-like and instructional. Use numbered lists for actionable frameworks. Bridge strategy and tactics. Be evergreen over urgent. (Matt Greener register) |

### Jargon Policy

Use industry terminology to signal expertise, but **always tie it to a business outcome**. Don't use jargon to sound smart—use it because it's the most precise term. When in doubt, use plain English. As Raaja Nemani notes: "It's not as bad as over-using acronyms to make yourself sound smart."

### Words to Avoid

| Avoid | Reason | Use Instead |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Game-changing, revolutionary | Hype language without substance | Specific outcomes ("generated 1,000s of leads") |
| Synergy, leverage (as verbs) | Corporate speak | Plain English alternatives |
| Hustle, grind | Glorifies overwork vs. smart systems | Working structurally, systems-based approach |
| Disrupt/disruption | Overused and vague | "Move the bar," "raise the standard" |
| Easy, simple, just | Dismissive of real complexity | Be honest about what's involved |
| Amazing, incredible, exciting | Empty superlatives | Let results speak |

## Formatting Conventions

### Sentence Structure

- **Typical Length**: Short to medium. 10-20 words is the sweet spot. Break long sentences.
- **Complexity**: Simple and compound sentences. Avoid nested clauses.
- **Voice**: Active voice strongly preferred. "AI raises the bar" not "The bar is raised by AI."

### The "Power Triad" (Alice Hansen Pattern)

For enterprise B2B and executive-level content, use the **Rule of Three** to structure key concepts:

| Triad Type | Example | When to Use |
|------------|---------|-------------|
| **Strategy Triads** | "Brand, demand, and data-led execution" | Describing integrated approaches |
| **Quality Triads** | "Clarity, cohesion, and relevance" | Describing content/messaging standards |
| **Growth Triads** | "Acquisition, retention, and expansion" | Describing revenue stages |
| **Transformation Triads** | "From plateau to performance" | Describing before/after states |

This pattern signals executive-level thinking and makes complex ideas memorable. Use parallelism for emphasis: "We cut through noise. We enable buyers and sellers. We scale efficiency."

### Paragraph Structure

- **Length**: 1-3 sentences per paragraph. Dense walls of text lose readers.
- **Flow**: Each paragraph should have one clear point. Use transitions sparingly—let white space do the work.

### Lists

- **When to Use**: For steps, options, comparisons, or when listing 3+ items
- **Format**: Bullets for unordered items; numbers for sequential steps or rankings
- **Parallel Structure**: Required. Start each item with the same part of speech.

### Headers

- **Style**: Sentence case preferred ("How to hire a growth marketer" not "How To Hire A Growth Marketer")
- **Hierarchy**: H2 for major sections, H3 for subsections. Don't skip levels.
- **Formatting**: Bold for emphasis within paragraphs; headers for navigation

### Opening Patterns

Strong openings from the analyzed content:

1. **The Tension Hook**: Start with a conflict, myth, or misconception
   - Example: "Marketers aren't worried about AI. Here's what marketers heard during the last 20 years..."

2. **The Story Hook**: Open with a personal anecdote or failure
   - Example: "Our brand name was spelled incorrectly on the carton... instead of 'Bucketfeet,' it said 'Burcketfeet.'"

3. **The Vulnerability Hook**: Acknowledge shared struggle to lower defenses
   - Example: "If you happen to be one of the people caught up in the cuts, breathe."

4. **The Reframe Hook**: Counter popular opinion with a surprising take
   - Example: "I think efficient and strategic project management precludes the need for check-ins."

5. **The Identity Hook** (Alice Hansen): Open with who you are and what you do
   - Example: "I'm Alice Hansen... I architect revenue growth from plateau to performance."

6. **The Diagnostic Hook** (MarketerHire Brand): Open with a common failure mode
   - Example: "Launching without a GTM plan is like sailing without a map... You'll burn time, budget, and goodwill."

### Closing Patterns

How to end content effectively:

1. **The Implication Close**: Move from observation to action
   - Example: End a problem diagnosis by implying the solution (shipping, execution, decision)

2. **The Trust Transfer**: Connect expertise to offering
   - Example: "If people trust you, they'll trust what you sell."

3. **The Principle Close**: Distill to a memorable rule
   - Example: "Be original. Find your own voice, develop your own original content, make your point in your own way."

4. **The "Full Guide" CTA** (MarketerHire Brand): Position external links as the complete resource
   - Example: "Full guide here ->" or "If any of that hits close to home, the long version... is worth your scroll."

5. **The Triad Summary** (Alice Hansen): Close with a three-part synthesis
   - Example: "We don't just generate noise; we drive the clarity, cohesion, and relevance required to meet buying groups exactly where they are."

### Visual Elements

- **Emoji Usage**: Minimal to none. Use sparingly for emphasis on social only, never in long-form.
- **Formatting (Bold/Italic)**: Bold for key terms and emphasis. Italics for titles or introducing new concepts.
- **Line Breaks**: Use generously. White space aids comprehension, especially on mobile.

## Do's and Don'ts

### Do

1. **Anchor in Tension**
   - Start with a common myth or fear and dismantle it with context
   - Example: "AI won't replace marketers—all that actually happened was people needed more marketers"

2. **Lead with Story**
   - Use personal failures or concrete examples to humanize lessons
   - Example: "I still remember the panic I felt when I was fired at 26 years old"

3. **Take a Stance**
   - Every piece needs a "specific point of view." Don't be wishy-washy.
   - Example: "I wouldn't say I'm an authority, but I have a specific point of view"

4. **Use Plain English**
   - Avoid buzzwords. Say "teams are stuck" not "organizations are experiencing friction"
   - Example: "It's your why, it's your reason for being"

5. **Quantify Value**
   - Always tie advice to measurable outcomes
   - Example: "Generated 1,000s of leads," "measurable growth," "$100M+ campaigns"

### Don't

1. **Don't Doom-Monger**
   - Never frame AI or industry change as apocalyptic
   - Instead: Position change as "raising the bar" for skilled practitioners

2. **Don't Over-Sell**
   - Avoid salesy language. Let outputs speak for themselves.
   - Instead: Show results ("personalized landing pages") rather than make claims

3. **Don't Glorify Hustle**
   - Avoid language suggesting working harder is the solution
   - Instead: Focus on working structurally, with better systems

4. **Don't Be Vague**
   - Avoid generic advice like "communicate better"
   - Instead: Specify how ("via sprint recaps," "through a schema of scope")

5. **Don't Project Perfection**
   - Avoid the "untouchable founder" persona
   - Instead: Share battle scars and lessons from failure

6. **Don't Use Growth-Hack Slang** (Alice Hansen register)
   - Avoid: "hacks," "tricks," "viral," "crushing it"
   - Instead: Use architectural language—"orchestrate," "architect," "catalyze"

7. **Don't Focus on Tactics Without Strategy** (Alice Hansen register)
   - Don't write about *how* to set up an ad campaign
   - Instead: Write about *why* the campaign aligns with business strategy and revenue architecture

8. **Don't Mix Diagnostic with Instructional** (MarketerHire Brand vs. Matt Greener)
   - MarketerHire brand voice is urgent and diagnostic ("here's what's broken")
   - Matt Greener's voice is evergreen and instructional ("here's how to build")
   - Pick one register per piece—don't blend

## Example Content

### Example 1: Thought Leadership (Chris Toy Style)

A strong example that captures the contrarian-operator voice:

> "Marketers aren't worried about AI. Here's what marketers heard during the last 20 years of digital/internet upending our industry:
>
> - Search will make marketing obsolete
> - Social media will replace advertising
> - Programmatic will automate us all away
>
> All that actually happened was people needed more marketers and more advertising for ever-evolving new tactics. AI won't be any different for us."

**Why it works**: Opens with a contrarian hook that challenges conventional fear. Uses historical context as evidence. Confident but not arrogant. Plain English throughout.

### Example 2: Career Advice (Nikhil Arora Style)

> "If you happen to be one of the people caught up in the cuts, breathe. Yes, breathe. Trust me, I've been there. I still remember the panic I felt when I was fired at 26 years old. 'How will I pay my rent?' was the first thing that came to mind."

**Why it works**: Leads with empathy and vulnerability. Uses personal failure to build trust. Short, punchy sentences create urgency without panic. Direct address ("you") creates intimacy.

### Example 3: Operational Guidance (Aneesha Rao Style)

> "I think efficient and strategic project management precludes the need for check-ins. If everything is set up, then the work should be completed in the timeline set forth. With freelancers, the guardrails I implement depend entirely on the level of expertise. With an experienced content or design marketer, I let them have at it, and give a basic schema of project scope."

**Why it works**: Takes a mildly contrarian stance (systems > communication volume). Uses precise PM terminology. Calibrates advice by context. Calm, authoritative tone.

### Example 4: Enterprise Strategy (Alice Hansen Style)

> "To catalyze momentum in enterprise SaaS, we must stop chasing leads and start orchestrating decision engines. My work focuses on the alignment of brand, demand, and data-led execution. We don't just generate noise; we drive the clarity, cohesion, and relevance required to meet buying groups exactly where they are."

**Why it works**: Uses the triadic structure throughout. Elevated vocabulary ("catalyze," "orchestrate," "decision engines"). C-Suite focus on buying groups vs. leads. Architectural framing—marketing as a structural engine, not tactical execution.

### Example 5: Diagnostic LinkedIn Post (MarketerHire Brand Voice)

> "If your campaigns try to reach everyone, they'll resonate with no one. High-value customers are hiding in plain sight... Customer segmentation flips the script: you market to real groups with shared behaviors, needs, and triggers, not a faceless average."

**Why it works**: Opens with the pain point (wasted targeting). Uses signature phrase "hiding in plain sight." Diagnostic but solution-focused. Anti-fluff—says what segmentation *actually does* rather than using vague promises.

### Example 6: Educational Content (Matt Greener Style)

> "6 ways to make yourself a more powerful marketer:
> 1. Align your materials with sales team needs
> 2. Bridge the gap between strategy and execution
> 3. Build trust through financial literacy..."

**Why it works**: Uses numbered list structure. Mentor-to-mentee tone—instructional not urgent. Evergreen advice that doesn't expire. Bridges sales/marketing divide. Professional and diplomatic.

## Platform-Specific Guidelines

### LinkedIn

- **Post Length**: Medium-form (150-300 words) performs well. Can go longer for deep dives.
- **Formatting**: Use line breaks generously. Avoid walls of text. Lists work well.
- **Hook Style**: Lead with tension, story, or a counterintuitive take in the first line.
- **CTA Style**: Soft CTAs preferred. Invite engagement ("What's your take?") over hard sells.
- **Voice**: The "Consultant-Operator"—diagnostic, helpful, solution-focused.

### Twitter/X

- **Thread Style**: Open strong, make each tweet standalone, build to insight.
- **Standalone Tweet Style**: Punchy, contrarian, or curated insight with additive commentary.
- **Engagement Style**: Amplify other smart operators. Add value when retweeting, don't just share.
- **Voice**: The "Smart Friend"—quick, witty, occasionally irreverent. Less polished than LinkedIn.

### Blog/Long-form

- **Structure**: Clear headers, scannable sections, table of contents for 2000+ word pieces.
- **Depth**: Comprehensive and reference-worthy. These are "canonical" documents.
- **CTAs**: Natural integration at end. Product mention should flow from content, not interrupt it.
- **Voice**: The "Professor"—thorough, structured, authoritative but accessible.

### Email/Newsletter

- **Tone**: Personal and direct. Write to one person, not a list.
- **Length**: Respect inbox. Get to the point.
- **Value**: Lead with insight, not ask. Every email should teach something.
